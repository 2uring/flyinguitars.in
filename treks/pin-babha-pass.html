Trek 02

Pin Babha Pass

Route
Kafnu to Mud Village
Crossover
Kinnaur to Spiti
Distance
~50 km
Max Altitude
~4,890 m
Difficulty
Challenging

If Kashmir Great Lakes is the trek that seduces you, Pin Babha Pass is the one that tests you. This isn't a manicured trail with signposts and tea stalls. It's a raw, remote crossing from the lush green valleys of Kinnaur into the stark, lunar moonscape of Spiti — two worlds separated by a single, knife-edge pass at nearly 5,000 metres.

Kafnu to the pass

The trek begins at Kafnu, a small village in the Sutlej valley. The first few days are deceptively gentle — dense forests of deodar and pine, the sound of the Babha river echoing through narrow gorges, and the occasional shepherd's hut marking the trail. The green is overwhelming, almost tropical in its density.

Then the treeline ends. The valley opens up into vast glacial moraines — grey rock, grey sky, grey everything. The Babha river narrows to a glacial stream, and the trail becomes a series of boulder fields and loose scree. You're climbing into a world that feels increasingly stripped of everything non-essential.

The pass

Pin Babha Pass at ~4,890 metres isn't dramatic in the way you'd expect. There's no sweeping panorama waiting for you at the top — just a narrow saddle between two ridges, prayer flags snapping in a wind that hasn't stopped since you left the treeline. But the feeling of standing there, knowing that the world on the other side looks nothing like the one you just walked through, is unlike anything else.

You walk out of green and into grey. Out of forests and into desert. The pass doesn't separate two valleys — it separates two realities.

Into Spiti

The descent into Spiti is a study in contrasts. Where Kinnaur was lush, Spiti is barren. Where the Babha valley was narrow and enclosed, the Pin valley opens up into an impossibly wide, arid expanse — ochre and rust-coloured cliffs, dry river beds, and a sky so clear it feels like you can see the curvature of the earth.

The trail ends at Mud Village in the Pin Valley — one of the highest inhabited villages in Spiti. From there, you're in a different world entirely. The crossing is complete. You've walked from one Himachal Pradesh into another, and neither of them is the same after.
