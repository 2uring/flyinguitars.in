About

Engineer by day.
Artist by instinct.

I'm Faiz Haq — a Senior Software Development Engineer at AMD, where I work at the intersection of high-performance computing and cutting-edge silicon. With a Masters in Computer Science from IIT Hyderabad, I bring the same rigour I apply to engineering into everything I create outside of it.

Under the alias flyinguitars, I live a parallel life — crafting tones through Neural DSP's Archetype Asato and Archetype Rabea, chasing golden hour with a camera, trekking through the Himalayas every chance I get, and unwinding on the football pitch. A Chelsea supporter since childhood — through every era, no questions asked.

Music

Tone is
everything.

The electric guitar is my first language. From ambient swells and soaring leads to tight, percussive riffs — every note is shaped with intention. I believe tone isn't just about gear; it's about feel, dynamics, and the space between the notes.

My signal chain runs through Neural DSP, where two plugins form the backbone of my sound. Whether I'm recording at home, jamming, or producing content for YouTube — these are the tools that translate what I hear in my head into what the world hears through the speakers.

Plugin
Archetype: Asato
Vintage-voiced cleans, rich overdrives, and soaring lead tones — organic, responsive, and full of character.
Plugin
Archetype: Rabea
Warm crunch, massive rhythm tones, and ambient post-rock textures in a single suite.
Photography

Chasing light,
one frame at a time.

Photography, for me, started with skies — those fleeting moments when the clouds arrange themselves into something you'll never see again. It grew into a practice of patience: waiting for the right light, the right shadow, the right silence in a frame.

From golden hour landscapes and moody street frames to candid portraits — my work lives across two handles. @faiskies_ for the personal, contemplative eye. @flyinguitars for where music and visuals converge.

The Himalayas

Called by the
mountains.

There's a version of me that only exists above the treeline — lighter, quieter, more honest. The Himalayas aren't just a destination; they're a reset. From multi-day alpine crossings through Kashmir to solo walks through forgotten Himachali villages, the mountains have shaped how I see everything else. Every trek is a conversation with silence, altitude, and yourself.

Featured Trek

Kashmir Great Lakes

Sonamarg to Naranag · ~72 km · Done twice

Seven alpine lakes set in a corridor of wildflower meadows and snow-dusted passes. The kind of landscape that makes you go back — and I did. Twice. Each time the lakes looked different. Each time I came back different.

Featured Trek

Pin Babha Pass

Kafnu to Mud Village · Spiti crossover

A raw, remote crossing from the lush Kinnaur valley into the stark moonscape of Spiti. Glacial moraines, river crossings, and high-altitude solitude — the kind of trek that strips everything down to what matters.

The village trails
of Himachal

Not every walk needs a summit. Some of the most profound days I've had in the mountains were on unmarked trails between Himachali villages — crossing wooden bridges over glacial rivers, drinking chai with shepherds, sleeping under skies untouched by light pollution. These are the hikes that made me fall in love with the Himalayas in the first place.
